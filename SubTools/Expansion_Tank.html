System Parameters
Enter the total system volume and operating temperatures. Typical fill temperature is the coldest the system will see (usually ambient ~50–70 °F).
Pressure Settings
Static height is the vertical distance from the tank to the highest point in the system. The fill pressure is auto-calculated from static head + 5 psi, but you can override it.
Fluid Properties
Select water or glycol solution. Higher glycol concentrations expand more and require larger tanks. Above 50% is unusual for HVAC — verify with your project requirements.

⚠ High Glycol Concentration
Above 50% glycol is unusual for HVAC systems. Verify with project specs. High concentrations significantly increase expansion volume and reduce heat transfer efficiency.
⚠ Pressure Range Too Narrow
Fill pressure is within 10 psi of the relief valve setting. This leaves very little operating range and may cause the relief valve to weep. Consider a higher relief valve setting or reducing static height with a different tank location.
⚠ Large Temperature Differential
ΔT exceeds 150 °F. This is atypical for standard HVAC systems — verify your fill and operating temperatures. High ΔT increases expansion volume substantially.
⚠ Invalid Pressure Configuration
The operating pressure is at or below the fill pressure. Check that the relief valve setting is high enough and the safety margin is appropriate.
⚠ PEX / Plastic Piping
PEX has a much higher coefficient of thermal expansion than metal. This increases the tank size. Ensure PEX is rated for the maximum operating temperature in your system.
Typical Design Conditions
System Type Tfill Tmax Prv Notes
Fill temperature is the coldest the system fluid will reach, including seasonal shutdown. Max operating temperature is the hottest during normal operation — not the boiler high limit.
When Things Are Not Typical

Fill temp below 32 °F (0 °C): Only valid with glycol. Pure water would freeze. The tool will warn you, but double-check your freeze protection concentration.

Relief valve under 30 psig (207 kPa): Unusual except for very low-rise buildings or open systems. May leave too little acceptance room — the tank gets very large.

Glycol above 50%: Dramatically increases expansion. Also raises viscosity and reduces heat transfer. Rarely needed unless protecting to -40 °F (-40 °C) or below.

PEX in high-temp systems: PEX expands ~10× more than steel. Fine for radiant floor and domestic, but verify temperature rating. Most PEX is limited to 180–200 °F (82–93 °C).

Very tall buildings (100+ ft / 30+ m static): Fill pressure will be high, potentially consuming most of the relief valve headroom. Consider placing the tank at an intermediate elevation, or use a pressurised make-up unit instead of a standard tank.
